Inpatient Waveform Data

This brief describes a new waveform data source being developed by the ICU-Heart team as a collaboration with DataLoch, the data service formed as a partnership between NHS Lothian and the University of Edinburgh (https://dataloch.org/about-us/faqs).

The Data

The waveform data is being collected in real time from patient bedside monitors in the Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh, and will be extended to cover the rest of NHSL ICUs this year. This data is comprised of high-resolution signals including multi-channel ECG, invasive blood pressure, pulse plethysmography and more, with these waveforms sampled at up to 250Hz.

Currently the data is being collected across all RIE critical care and cardiology beds with the potential of expanding this to cover other clinical areas such as theatres, the acute medical unit, and parts of the emergency department, including resus. The volumes of data being collected are vast with the nearly 80 beds across critical care and cardiology streaming on average 7 signals of continuous waveform data. This yields approximately 300,000 hours of waveform data per month, or expressed in heartbeats 2.5B per year.

We believe this is the first project in the UK that is collecting routine waveform data at a large scale for research purposes. We are very excited about the potential for this data to answer clinical questions, with this type of data being ideal for the application of machine or deep learning approaches.

The Progress

We are currently archiving this waveform data whilst we develop a robust pipeline to process and store it for long term use, with plans to deploy this pipeline by the end of the year. This long-term data will then be query-able for specific patients or cohorts, where it can then be linked to other DataLoch patient datasets such as demographics, diagnoses, medications or laboratory results, including those derived from primary care records. This data can then be provided to researchers as a pseudonymised dataset in a sandboxed environment, where it could be used to tackle specific research problems or questions.
